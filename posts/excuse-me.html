"Excuse Me" Isn't a License to Kill

One of the more frustrating types of people you encounter in public are those who yell out "excuse me" like it's a free pass for everything. Politeness isn't just about using the right words; it's about first finding a way to not inconvenience others. Can you go around the obstacle, even if it requires ten extra steps, instead of yelling "excuse me" to a grandpa who happens to be in your way?

Yelling out "excuse me" doesn't inherently make you a polite person—often, it just masks entitlement. A truly polite person is someone who tries really hard to find a way not to yell "excuse me" for every minor inconvenience they face.

We are all just part of this journey called life. You don't matter entirely. I don't matter entirely. The world does not circle around you, nor does it circle around me. Unfortunately, a lack of situational awareness is all too common. Here are some personal experiences that add context to what I mean.

There was one time I was in a Costco parking lot, loading stuff into my car while holding my baby in my arm. Suddenly, I hear an "excuse me" and turn to see a woman expecting me to move out of the way for her and her cart. The strange thing is, we were in a parking lot. She could have simply rolled her cart out around me. But applying basic logic seemed to be too much work for her; instead, she demanded that I adapt to her path.

I also see this when I walk my dogs. People often expect me to get out of their way simply because I have dogs with me. My dogs love people, but if you are the one who actively avoids them, shouldn't you be the one to alter your course? You don't own the public space. At some point, I stopped getting out of their way just to observe the reaction. Some people would make faces, reluctantly step an inch away, and make exaggerated gestures as if my dogs were vicious.

Recently, my family and I visited a newly opened grocery store. It was packed. There were so many people that I literally couldn't move. Then, I hear a little kid saying "excuse me." Didn't he see the situation? It was completely stalled, and I had nowhere else to go. When I didn't move—because I couldn't—his mom told him, "Next time you have to speak louder." It really highlighted a lack of situational awareness, proving that entitlement is often a learned behavior.
